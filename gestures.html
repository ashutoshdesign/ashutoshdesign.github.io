Exploring Gestures in VR

Gestures are a way of interacting that have become popular for Virtual Reality systems. With a rich gestural heritage of India, culturally compliant interactions can be designed using gestures. But would Indian users accept such a novel technique?

This is an ongoing project since December'17, under the guidance of Prof. Pradeep Yammiyavar, Director, Usability Engineering Lab, Indian Institute of Technology Guwahati.

Project Overview

"The one where I played a researcher, designer and developer"

In India, gestures form an essential part of the rich non-verbal body communication spectrum. Gesture-based interactions can overcome the language barrier posed by the diversity in culture and pronounced digital illiteracy in India. However, acceptance of gestures among Indian users remains underexplored.

In this project, I designed and prototyped interactions based on hand gestures, natural-object interaction and headgaze in VR environment. I performed a comparative study in the context of online financial transaction to evaluate user preference, workload and usability. I analyzed this data using statistical and thematic analysis and derived conclusive findings.

Interative design process

The goal of the project was to obtain valuable insights on how users perceive gestures as a method of interaction in VR. So, we prototyped an experience with gestures which helped reveal those insights.

Prototyping

“Tinkering with Leap Motion”

Leap Motion is a sensor device that supports hand and finger movement as input. I was curious to explore the numerous possibilities of this device. So, I started the project by prototyping two interfaces based on gestural interaction and natural-object interaction for number-entry. I used Leap Motion with Unity3D to develop the prototypes for HTC Vive. This was also my initial exposure to Virtual Reality (VR).

Conceptualization

“How would transactions in VR look like?”

Based on findings from my literature review, I conceptualized two VR interfaces based on the two interaction techniques for the comparative study. The context of comparative study I chose was a quotidian task- an online transaction after purchasing any product or service online. I broke down the task into 3 parts- 1) Selection of mode of payment, 2) Selection of Credit Card and 3) PIN Entry.

Gestural Interaction

Natural Object Interaction

1. Selection of mode of payment

  • Body-referenced menus for textual items for good visibility
  • Menu items as labels on fingertips on facing palm towards headset
  • Selection using pinch gesture followed by sound feedback
  • Active and inactive labels interchangeable using "more" label on pinky finger
  • Menu items appear on a body-referenced panel with two rows of buttons
  • Panel appears on facing palm towards headset
  • Selection is done by ‘pressing and releasing’ the menu item with index finger of dominant hand
  • Pseudo-haptic feedback using sound and change in color of the button

2. Selection of credit card

  • World-referenced menu with visual items (credit cards) arranged in a curved rectangular arrangement
  • Cards are labeled 1-6 and a card is selected via finger counting gesture, followed by feedback sound.
  • Extend the number of fingers to denote the desired numeral
  • World-referenced menu with visual items (credit cards) arranged in a curved rectangular arrangement
  • The relevant card is selected by ‘pressing and releasing’ it using the index finger of either hand
  • Pseudo-haptic feedback using sound and change in color of the button

3. Entering 4-digit PIN

  • The selected card is displayed and PIN is entered using finger-count gestures
  • A panel similar to that in Task 1 appears as the left-palm faces the headset

“What are finger-count gestures again?”

Literatures say that finger-count gestures have performed well for menu selection tasks in a desktop environment and hold potential for numerical input in VR environment as well. Also, they have been suggested for hands-free numerical input.

Since multiple practices are followed in India to gesture a numerical digit, the design of finger-count gestures was not restricted to a single set of hand poses. It considered the number of extended fingers held by either or both of the dominant and non- dominant hands.

“Additional gesture interactions”

Apart from finger-count gestures, other common gestures enabled other functionalities. A thumbs-up gesture of the right hand was used to confirm a selection whereas a cancellation is exhibited using the index fingers of both hands joined in the form of a cross.

User Study

“The first time I conducted a usability study, I had no clue”

A within-group study with 20 users was conducted to compare the two interactions. Undergraduate students aged between 18-22 years exhibiting a tech-savvy behavior volunteered for the experiment. None of the users had prior experience with any VR application. However they all were proficient in making online transaction using smartphones and desktops. The experiment duration ranged from 40-50 mins.

Independent variables include (1) Two VR interfaces and (2) Past experience of participants in VR. Dependent variables include (1) Task completion time, (2) Workload and (3) System Usability.

“Findings from statistical analysis”

I used statistical analysis methods to derice results from the data. The data was found to be normally distrbuted. I used paired-t test on SPSS anaysis software to compare means to find if the differences were statistically significant. Interesting inferences were drawn from the audio-recorded interviews.

“What challenges did I face during this experiment?”

I was met with numerous challenges while conducting the above study. The technical constraints posed by the Leap Motion often frustrated accurate data readings. This may also have biased the users' preferences. Also, the interface was more complex than required. This increased the learning time and hence study time as both techniques. Further, I realized that I needed a new set of metrics to evaluate user preference for a culturally diverse setting like India.

Literature Review

“Digging the literature again”

Based on the findings from the previous experiment, I decided to reduce the complexity of my experiment. I shifted my focus completely on the PIN entry task to evaluate user acceptance. Of the numerous insights I obtained from my review, the following two drove the rest of my study-

"Higher performance and perceived ease of use and usefulness fasten the acceptance of a new technology in India”

“Headgaze pointing has been found to be intuitive and a preferred technique for hands-free text-entry”

Conceptualization

“Learning from the mistakes”

The second study also consisted of two interfaces but involved headgaze interaction instead of natural-object interaction.

The headgaze interface consisted of a regular number keypad derived from 2D GUIs. The input was given by pointing at the desired digit using headgaze with a dwell time of 1000ms (recommended duration for novice users).

Number-entry in the gestural interface remained unchanged except for the change in feedback. The acceptance of a digit was dwell-based where participants held a gesture for 1000ms (denoted by a circular progress bar).

Prototyping

“Wizard of Oz with Leap motion”

Prototyping headgaze-based interface was fairly simple using Unity3D and Cardboard SDK. But how do I circumvent the technical constraints of the Leap Motion controller? So, I got out my developer mindset and put my "design-thinking" cap on. Wizard of Oz was clearly the way out.


While the Leap Motion enabled visual feedback of hands in VR, I played the wizard and controlled all the functions triggered inside the scene. The experimental set-up was such that I could watch and control the ongoing VR scenes using my computer (I wouldn't delineate the specifics of the setup here). I meticulously timed and triggered all functions using shortcut keys on my keyboard. This way I contrived to deliver a seamless user experience eliminating any bias or erroneous readings.

User Study

“Usability study Part II”

A within-group study with 20 users was conducted to compare the two interactions. Undergraduate students aged between 18-22 years exhibiting a tech-savvy behavior volunteered for the experiment. None of the users had prior experience with any VR application. The experiment duration ranged from 20-30 mins.

Independent variables included (1) Two VR interfaces for PIN-entry and (2) Past experience of participants in VR. Dependent variables included (1) Task completion time, (2) Perceived ease of use and (3) Perceived usefulness.

In this study, I ensured the following two things while conducting the study. This, I believed, would heavily influence my data.

  1. Sufficient time and practice be given to users before the actual task
  2. Participants be provided with a well articulated task-scenario sheet

“Collating quantitative and qualitative findings”

Literatures say that quantitative metrics like performance and perceived ease of use and usefulness positively affect the acceptance of technology in India. In this study, I expand the scope of this model by collating these metrics with the qualitative responses. I posited here that statistical findings alone wouldn't provide valuable insights to assess user preference in a diverse setting like India.


So, along with statistical analysis of quantitative data, I also performed a thematic analysis (inspired by grounded theory) on the interview data. The common emerging themes revealed many serendipitous insights.


Learnings Recap

This project was my first introduction to research into HCI which has helped me develop a good understanding of experiment design. This was also my rudimentary exposure to Virtual Reality. I learnt to design and prototype simple VR interfaces in Unity3D, Leap Motion and numerous other packages. This project has spurred by interest into exploring the domain of multimodal interaction techniques and designing meaningful interactions using them.
