Making Haptics Accessible

Decades of haptic device invention is buried in literature- effectively lost to device engineers and experience designers. How might we then make haptic devices easily accessible to the community?

This project was done between May-July 2018, under the guidance of Prof. Karon Maclean, Director, SPIN Lab, the University of British Columbia, Canada and Dr. Hasti Seifi, Postdoc Researcher, HI Department , Max Plack Institute for Intelligent Systems, Germany.

Project Overview

"Hi Karon, Nice to meet you!"

Haptipedia is an open source haptic device visualization library that improves accessibility to haptic devices for engineers and designers. It supports exploration, ideation and fabrication of haptic devices using interactive data visualizations.

My role was to research device attributes and design and prototype data-driven interfaces that can best support hapticians achieve their goals. I collaborated with Jessica Ip during my entire project under the close supervision of Dr. Hasti Seifi and other researchers from the University of British Columbia, Canada and Max Planck Institute for Intelligent Systems, Germany.

User Study

“Hasti, what are we looking for?”

The project started with us getting familiarized with the field of haptics and understanding the research question. The research question posed to us was-

“What device attributes and visual interfaces can best support hapticians in choosing a haptic device/platform for their intended applications?”


We figured it out early in the process that the device attributes we were looking for were the initial project requirements of hapticians. These requirements might be in the form of technical constraints or end user needs. To discover these requirements, we needed to understand the design process of hapticians. This formed the basis of our study methodology.

“Interview prep won’t even take a week!”

We structured the interview to encourage storytelling among participants. It was divided into three parts-

  1. Background of the participant
  2. Story of a previous project
  3. Protocol Analysis with prototype

Framing the questions for the interview was a carefully thought out process. We had to keep away from the “design jargon” as most hapticians aren’t familiar with these terms. I had never imagined that it could take upto 2 weeks to design a good user study.

User Study

“16 seems achievable”

Contrary to what the heading says, we interviewed 10 participants spread across a span of 5 weeks. We had interviewees with experience in both building devices and designing experiences with those devices.

“Jessica kick me if I go beyond an hour”

We conducted interviews both in-person and remotely via video conferencing (It was challenging!). Each interview lasted for about an hour for which participants received CA$15 Amazon e-gift card as an honorarium.

A participant during exploratory activity

Even Hasti used to join the interviews sometimes

After summarising their educational and work experience, the participants described one of their previous projects on the lines of motivation, requirements and pros and cons of explored haptic devices.

During the protocol analysis, the participants interacted with Haptipedia prototype to complete a task scenario and verbalized their thoughts. The task scenario was selecting a haptic device (for experience designers) or redesign it (for device engineers). We audio recorded them and screen recorded their interactions with the prototype.

“Story-telling helped us gather insights on attributes that hapticians are most interested in”

“Protocol analysis helped us find usability issues, evaluate existing attributes and work out user tasks”

Prototyping

“Rome wasn’t built in a week but the D3 prototype was!”

During the course of the interviews, we realised that the Tableau prototype wasn’t powerful enough and yielded repeated insights. We decided to switch our prototype to “D3.js”. But the challenge was- Could it be implemented in time for the remaining interviews?


While the team hunted for a professional, we started a small side project of our own. With Jessica’s help, I was able to build a working D3 prototype in a week’s time. This prototype supported features based on feedback obtained from the ongoing user study.

Analysis

“Coding data was more difficult than I had thought”

After the tedious task of transcribing the interviews, we used a thematic analysis influenced by grounded theory to analyze the data.


Guided by our research question, we, along with Hasti, set out to code the data using MAXQDA software separately. We used a hybrid of pre-set codes and open codes. We used memo writing and axial coding to connect the codes. After numerous discussions, we developed the initial themes around recurring device attributes, user wants/tasks and user behaviour.

Design

“Learning to design data visualizations”

This stage was particularly interesting for me as we collaborated with another researcher at UBC- Michael Oppermann, who has significant experience in vis design. Together with Michael, we learnt how to implement the “What-Why-How” vis design framework proposed by Prof. Tamara Munzner.

Michael teaching us the ways of a vis designer

Jessica and I having a hard time implementing the framework

“Why- Task Abstraction”

The most important part of this framework is to consider user wants/tasks obtained from user research in an abstract form. We called them “visualization tasks”. These tasks were fundamental in concisely identifying the underlying user goals which guided our design of vis and other elements in the interface.

An example showing narrowing down from user wants to abstract tasks

“Which attributes go in the vis, which ones in the filters”

While we brainstormed on the tasks, we also prioritized all the device attributes on the basis of their frequency of recurrence. This sorting of attributes along with the tasks guided the design of hierarchical filters, visualisations and other interface elements.

“Implementing the What-why-how framework”

Shown below is an example visualization from Haptipedia designed using the "What-Why-How" framework. This framework highly simplifies the vis design process and efficiently incorporates all user needs.

Learnings Recap

This project offered me a good exposure to international research in the field of HCI under two amazing mentors. I could not have utilised my summer in a more productive manner. I learnt how to work and communicate in a cross-cultural team. I also learnt how to conduct a well-structured user study and analyse data using the grounded theory method. This project also got me interested into the field of data visualization design which I would love to explore further.

Karon made sure that I get enough opportunities to interact closely with HCI researchers from academia and industry like U of Washington, U of Tokyo, Microsoft, Apple and Samsung. I look forward to future collaborations with my mentors and exposure to international research. P.S. The awesome hikes, food and culture made me fall in love with Vancouver.
