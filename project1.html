Understanding Users at 1mg

1mg.com, an e-commerce healthcare start-up, needed a deeper understanding of their userbase so that they could incorporate it in their product development process.

This project took place between May 2017 and July 2017, under the guidance of Mridu Mehta, Senior Interaction Designer at 1mg.

My Role

As a design research intern, I carried out extensive early stage user research using ethnographic research methods. I also analysed and mapped(empathy and journey mapping) the findings from my research. The insights generated from the analysis along with the mapping enabled us to model user personas, which was the aim of the project.

Process

We followed a process quite similar to Erin Sander’s 5-stage research learning spiral. We omitted “Hypotheses” stage to stay consistent with our goal of conducting user research without any bias or mental barrier.

Inspired from the research learning spiral, a five-step process for conducting user research, originated by Erin Sanders at Frog.

Objectives

What we needed to do

Our first step was to define our focus and develop a detailed research plan. Our main focii were “pharmacists” and “chronic patients”.

Understanding Pharmacists

  1. Gain better understanding of users and their behaviour and attitude.
  2. Observe their environment and how they interact with it and the people in it.
  3. Dig-out the “unknown-unknowns”.

Understanding Chronic Patients

  1. Gain better understanding of users suffering from chronic diseases with major focus on diabetes, cardiac and pulmonary diseases.
  2. Understand patient’s lifestyle, routine and disease management.
  3. Understand the challenges faced by patients.
Methods

The methods we used

To achieve more open-ended observations and insights, we decided to use “unscripted” methods of design ethnography where one observes users performing in their own environment.

Generally, there is a considerable difference between “what people say” and “what people do”. The methods described below conveniently bridge that gap.

Fly-on-the-wall Observation

I used this method to observe pharmacists in their stores. The final goal was to get data on how pharmacists interact with customers, how they manage their store and to get users’ expectations from pharmacists. FOTW observation was the perfect tool for the job.

Shadowing

I used "shadowing" to gather information on diabetics. The reason behind using this method was to dive deeper into the lives of diabetics and find insights which interviews in a constrained lab environment had failed to reveal.

Contextual Inquiry

I used this method to collect data on cardiac and pulmonary patients. Initially, we intended to use “shadowing” for these patients too. But early visits to the field revealed that the major challenges faced by these patients lay in “treatment and diagnosis” and not in “disease management”, hence, contextual inquiry.

Conduct

Conducting ethnographic study

“Conduct your research with an open mind” said Mridu, while prepping me for field research. We needed our research to be as open- ended as possible without any personal bias.

Observing Pharmacists

I would go to some random pharmacy in the city and convince the pharmacist to stay in his store for a day. I would then find a discreet spot in the store and start to record my observations.

“I observed 3 pharmacists in a span of 5 days spending about 5-6 hours with each.”

I used the “AEIOU” technique to observe participant’s activities, his interaction with customers and environment, artefacts etc. I mostly made notes and sketch notes to record my observations besides photographs, videos and audio recordings.

Photographs and Notes taken during FOTW observation of pharmacists

Following diabetics on their routine check-up

I visited a dedicated clinic for diabetes patients where they consult with a diabetologist, a dietician and a therapist. I followed diabetics on their journey to their routine visit to the clinic.

“I shadowed 3 diabetics in a span of 2 days spending about 2-3 hours with each.”

I also conducted a small interview with the patients as well as their caregivers after the exercise to clarify any doubts and gain a deeper insight into their lifestyle. I also interacted with diabetologists, dieticians and consultants but found their views to be a little biased. Note-taking and audio recording methods were extensively used to record observations.

Participant undergoing a test(L) and Notes(R) taken during shadowing diabetics

Visiting cardiology and pulmonary departments at aiims

I visited the AIl India Institute of Medical Sciences, New Delhi, to observe and interact with cardiac and pulmonary patients. I prepared a small questionnaire with open ended questions before hand to guide me through the interview.

“I interviewed 2 cardiac patients, 2 pulmonary patients and 2 caregivers spending about 1.5-2 hours with each.”

I interviewed the patients and their caregivers following a semi-structured interview fomat, with major focus on the challenges they faced during disease diagnosis. I recorded all the interviews on a GoPro camera.

Synthesis

Organisation and analysis of results

After each productive field-visit, I would pour out all my findings and have a quick insight-generation session with Mridu.

Analysing 15 hours of observation data on pharmacists

The volume of data captured from FOTW observation of pharmacists was immense. Analysing this data was a mammoth task. I formulated insights from this data and delivered a keynote presentation to the team.

Using journey and empathy maps

To get deeply into the lives of chronic patients, we decided to make empathy and journey maps.

I poured down all the data from shadowing and contextual interviews onto post-it notes, filling walls after walls. Then I rearranged and regrouped the into the most logical order of events in the journey.

Filtering data in stages

Following the “2.5-D sketch theory”, I filtered the data from the walls onto paper and then finally digitised them to deliver final journey and empathy maps, consumable for the whole team.

“This helped us map users’ emotions, thoughts and painpoints at each point of their journey and find potential areas of design intervention”

Modelling User Personas

We ended up with 4 Journery maps, 2 Empathy maps and tons of insights at the end of the research phase. From this data, we developed 4 “Provisional user personas”.

"These personas were to serve as models for all ongoing projects and as inspirations for new ideas, driving future projects."

Learnings Recap

I learnt to use various qualitative research methods to conduct on-field user research and craft insights from it. I also learnt the tools of the trade like contextual inquiries, user journey maps and empathy maps and persona development.

“By the last week of my internship, insights from my research were already being incorporated into the product.”

The project had many challenges but the most difficult part was to get the product managers and developers involved into the lives of the users to get them to empathise with the users. So to make them discard their biases, I designed small games from my research and also conducted a few ideation sessions with them.

This game involved completing a user's story. Teams were briefed a little about the user and given a few clues from his journey. They were then given a shuffled set of cards which contained excerpts from the story. They had to rearrange the cards in an attempt at replicating the story.

"How might we" questions were framed from the research insights. These questions were then passed around within the whole team where people built around each other's ideas.

"We are now better informed about our user base thanks to his effort and have incorporated it in our product development process. During the period of his internship program with us, we found him to be a quick learner, inquisitive and a hardworking person."

- Mridu Mehta, Senior Interaction Designer at 1mg
