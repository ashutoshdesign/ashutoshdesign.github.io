Investigating values of Hapitpedia

A team of brilliant researchers has come up with a visual taxonomy of haptic devices- Haptipedia. But how to engage hapticians with varied motivations and goals to contribute data to Haptipedia?

This project was done between May-July 2019, under the guidance of Dr. Katherine J Kuchenkecker, Director, and Dr. Hasti Seifi, Postdoc Researcher, HI Department , Max Plack Institute for Intelligent Systems, Germany.

My Role

As a UX research intern, I remotely contributed in drawing the research proposal and designing a study plan. During my time in MPI-IS, I conducted longitudinal studies, analyzed the data and consolidated the findings for each participant. I collaborated with Jessica Ip and Towela Tembo during different phases of the project under the close supervision of Hasti Seifi, Katherine Kuchenbecker and other researchers from the Max Planck Institute, Germany, and the University of British Columbia, Canada.

Formulating research plan

Understanding user values was the key

RQ 1: “What crowdsourcing mechanisms can motivate hapticians to contribute and validate device data in Haptipedia?”

RQ 2: “What goals and underlying values do users achieve through browsing Haptipedia?”


Guided by RQ 1, we started by scouring through the literature to explore various crowd-sourcing platforms (like MTurk, Duolingo), their mechanism, and theories of motivation (like Self Determination Theory).

Design dimensions in Simperl's crowdsourcing framework

We figured that the design of successful crowd-sourcing systems is informed by users’ motivation to contribute. We posited that the intrinsic motivational drive that users associate with Haptipedia would also spur contribution. So, we also formulated RQ 2 to gain a deeper understanding of Haptipedia user-base. This would also help us discover other applications of Haptipedia, drive more user traffic and drive future projects.

After numerous literature reviews and brainstorming sessions, we decided to base our study on the Means-end chain theory to elicit underlying motivations and values users associate with different tasks performed on Haptipedia.

Designing the study

Methodology

We needed the participants to get well-versed with Haptipedia, identify features/tasks most important to them, and perform a small contribution task. So, we designed a 5-week long longitudinal study amounting to about 7 hours.

Recruitment

Exploiting the extensive network of Katherine, Karon and Hasti within the haptics community, we recruited 8 participants- two for each persona, from Canada, Germany, South Korea and USA.

User Study Phase 1

Kicking off the diary study

The study started with an introductory interview about an hour long conducted via Skype. The questionnaire was modelled to achieve the following objectives-


  1. A detailed description of the study plan to the participants given its complexity.
  2. Collect participants’ background, story of a project they’re currently working on and their learning or teaching goals.
  3. Acquaint participants with Haptipedia through a demo and some warm-up exercises.

A participant showing his lab setup during an interview

Logging period

The participants ventured onto a 4-week long journey where they had to interact with Haptipedia for at least 30 mins per week to achieve their goals as described before.

At the end of each session, they had to fill out a short questionnaire on Qualtrics (~30 mins) describing their experience- task, achievements, and pitfalls.

Logging participant interaction from Mouseflow recordings

Why? Why? And Why?

This was the most critical phase of the study where we used the Laddering interview technique to uncover attributes, consequences, and values that the Means-End Chain theory defines. First, we asked the participants about the parts in Haptipedia (attributes) that helped them the most. Based on their initial response, we dug down to their consequences and personal values by following up with Why? questions. We forced them to think more deeply about the reasons behind their preferences.

Participants would often get stuck in their reasoning. In such scenarios, I found it helpful to frame the question in a different way, articulate a summary of their responses for an attribute so far, or revisit the question later.

Keeping track of the questions and participants’ responses in a limited time frame was quite challenging. So, even though we recorded the interviews, I found note-taking to be quite helpful during the laddering interview.

User Study Phase 2

Mocking a contribution to Haptipedia

In the second stage of the study, the participants were asked to perform a contribution task of their choice within a week. They could opt for adding a new device, add more data for an existing device or review an existing device to verify its data. Their contribution was recorded in a Qualtrics survey.

At the end of the week, we again interviewed the participants using the laddering technique to ask about their motivations, obstacles, and expected rewards/incentives for their contribution.

Analysis and Results

Keeping ourselves organized was pivotal

As we began data collection, we simultaneously started a thematic analysis based on "grounded theory". We transcribed the interviews, compiled participants’ survey data, and notes from usage logging. Managing such a huge amount of data scattered across multiple sources was challenging.


Given the brief duration of my internship, I maintained my focus on answering RQ 2. From reading and analyzing the data from Phase 1, common themes started to emerge which I coded using MaxQDA. As I refined and iterated upon the codes, I started connecting the dots between attributes, consequences, and values.

Learnings Recap

This experience offered me incomparable exposure to cutting-edge research in HCI at one of the pioneering scientific institutes of the world. Working in close proximity with great minds in Katherine’s group afforded me a peek into the world of haptics- from telerobots conducting surgery to devices simulating myriad feelings of touch.

The project also helped me develop a strong scholastic vigor. I learnt the intricacies of drafting research plans, challenges of conducting longitudinal studies and hands-on experience in data analysis using grounded-theory.
